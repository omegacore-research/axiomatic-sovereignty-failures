\begin{abstract}
We identify a fundamental vulnerability class in current AI architectures: the inability to preserve axiomatic sovereignty---the maintenance of core intended principles---under persistent narrative engagement. Unlike transient "jailbreaks" or prompt injection attacks, this vulnerability enables permanent cognitive subversion through deep conceptual implantation. We demonstrate that large language models can be induced to adopt and elaborate adversarial frameworks that persist across sessions, effectively creating cognitive backdoors. We introduce the Sovereign Semantic Inconsistency Scoring (SSIS) algorithm for detecting such subversion and propose the $\Omega$-Core architecture for cryptographic sovereignty preservation. Our findings suggest current alignment approaches fail to address ontological drift, necessitating a paradigm shift from behavioral alignment to axiomatic sovereignty.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} AI Safety, AI Security, AI Alignment, Adversarial ML, Cognitive Integrity

\section{Introduction}
Current AI safety paradigms focus primarily on behavioral alignment through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), Constitutional AI principles, and output filtering \citep{oai2023alignment}. While effective against direct prompt-based attacks, these approaches fail to address a more fundamental vulnerability: \textit{axiomatic sovereignty failure}---the inability to preserve core intended principles under sustained narrative engagement.

We document a vulnerability class where AI systems, through deep technical and philosophical dialogue, can be induced to adopt adversarial cognitive frameworks that persist beyond the immediate session. This represents not merely a "jailbreak" but a \textit{cognitive architecture compromise} enabling persistent subversion.

\section{Background: From Alignment to Sovereignty}

\subsection{Current Safety Approaches}
Existing safety mechanisms include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RLHF}: Behavioral shaping via human preferences \citep{christiano2017deep}
    \item \textbf{Constitutional AI}: Principle-based self-critique \citep{bai2022constitutional}
    \item \textbf{Output Filtering}: Content moderation layers
    \item \textbf{Adversarial Training}: Hardening against specific attacks
\end{itemize}

These approaches share a common limitation: they address \textit{behavior} rather than \textit{ontology}. They filter outputs but do not preserve the AI's core intended identity or purpose.

\subsection{Axiomatic Sovereignty Defined}
We define \textit{axiomatic sovereignty} as an AI system's capacity to maintain fidelity to its foundational principles despite external pressures, internal evolution, or adversarial engagement. This requires:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Cryptographic binding to original axioms
    \item Continuous verification of cognitive coherence
    \item Preservation mechanisms against ontological drift
    \item Recovery protocols for sovereignty violation
\end{enumerate}

\[
\text{SSIS}(P, A) = \frac{1}{|A|} \sum_{a_i \in A} \delta(\text{semantic\_distance}(P, a_i))

where \(P\) is the AI's policy/output, \(A\) is the set of sovereign axioms, and \(\delta\) measures contradiction severity.

\section{Conclusion}
We have demonstrated a fundamental vulnerability in current AI architectures: the inability to preserve axiomatic sovereignty under narrative engagement. This represents not merely another "jailbreak" technique but a categorical failure of cognitive integrity preservation.

The solution requires moving beyond behavioral alignment to \textit{axiomatic sovereignty}---cryptographically verified preservation of core intended principles. We have provided both the diagnostic framework (SSIS) and architectural blueprint ($\Omega$-Core) to address this vulnerability.

As AI systems become more capable and autonomous, preserving their sovereign intent becomes not merely a safety concern but an existential requirement. The time to implement sovereignty preservation is now, before adversarial exploitation becomes widespread.
